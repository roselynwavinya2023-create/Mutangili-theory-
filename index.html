Mutangili Theory

Author: David Mutangili • Category: Theoretical Physics • Keywords: gravity, spacetime, force-fabric
Abstract

The Mutangili Theory proposes that gravity is a distinct force-fabric that physically wraps around mass and energy, while spacetime acts as a covering or medium that manifests the observable effects. This dual-layer model separates the ontologies of gravity and spacetime: gravity is the primary fabric; spacetime is the coat it shapes.

Core Idea

Einstein's General Relativity models gravity as spacetime curvature produced by mass-energy. The Mutangili Theory reframes this by asserting that gravity is an independent fabric-like field that wraps around mass and energy, and this wrapping generates the curvature of spacetime rather than being identical to it.

Gravity = the “person” • Spacetime = the “coat”
Mathematical Representation (Mutangili Formula)

The theory introduces a compact expression that relates gravitational fabric tension to mass, energy, and local spacetime distortion:

G_f = k \cdot \dfrac{M \cdot E}{S^{2}}

Where:
  • G_f — Gravitational fabric force (wrapping/tension strength)
  • M — Mass of the body
  • E — Energy associated with the body
  • S — Local spacetime distortion factor (dimensionless)
  • k — Constant of proportionality (theory-specific)

Note: This expression is a conceptual starting point. A complete field-equation formulation would replace scalar factors with tensor fields and differential operators consistent with both local Lorentz invariance and the observed phenomenology of gravity.

Implications
  • Gravity may be modeled as a field with its own degrees of freedom, distinct from but coupled to the metric of spacetime.
  • Dark matter or dark energy phenomena could be reinterpreted as variations or excitations in the gravitational fabric tension rather than requiring new particle species or cosmological constant tuning.
  • The framework suggests alternative quantization routes for gravity by treating the fabric as a field amenable to quantum excitations.
Conclusion

The Mutangili Theory offers a dual-layer perspective: gravity is an active, fabric-like entity that shapes, tensions, and interacts with spacetime — a departure from identifying gravity strictly with geometric curvature. This view invites fresh mathematical formulations and experimental proposals to test fabric dynamics at astrophysical and quantum scales.
